**Tests**

The way to test closures is to solve the equations that use the closures and compare the results with reality (experimental data), other closures and references. As part of my open source species_limits.py project, I included the CVODE integrator from the excellent scikit-sundae library to solve the ODEs for each species. I used the same mixing parameters and calculations as the 2025 paper from Baldyga's group, including the analytical solution for intensity of segregation, allowing the variance of f to be calculated easily at each time (or position).  I also included the ability to perform an epsilon "sweep" from very slow mixing (1.0E-6 W/kg) to very fast mixing (1.0E+6 W/kg) with the same fixed reaction kinetics. That allows exploration of any closure over the full range of mixing timescales from almost completely segrategated reaction conditions through to almost completely well-mixed reaction conditions.  We are looking for closures that behave correctly at both ends and especially in the middle where both mixing and kinetics are influential and interact strongly. Each integration proceeds to 99.9% consumption of the limiting reactant; that is A in this case, fed through stream 1 with f=1.

**Baldyga's limits**

Baldyga (1994) uses a closure based on a pair of infinitely fast reaction subsets for both competitive-consecutive and competitive-parallel reactions.  In the competitive-consecutive case, he discusses how $f_{s}$ moves as the product of the first reaction is produced and the second reaction is enabled to proceed. In the 2009 and 2025 papers from his group on the full 5-reaction system, the movement of $f_{s}$ is directly calculated from the amount of R (or S) already produced during reaction. That is a powerful idea as a means of identifying a unique infinitely fast reaction limit. C(f) for all species can then be obtained by interpolation between that limit and the no-reaction / mixing line.  I implemented a somewhat more generalized version of this method as one closure option in species_limits.py. Full mathematical details of my implementation are available [here]().  

**Simulation results**

I tried to generalize the published (2009 and 2025) approach beyond the azo-coupling example and one idea was to identify reaction subsets that were missing one potential product; those subsets were then weighted by the amount of other (not missing) products they produced to calculate the moving limit.  This used three limits instead of two and missed one of the limits used in the 2025 paper so is not identical.  I also enabled an option for the user to stipulate/ hardwire precisely which subsets to combine; that enabled me to reproduce the method in the paper exactly, I believe.  I am in contact with Baldyga's group and am checking additional details.

Other diagnostics include the simulation CPU time (blend_fs is generally fast) and the number of times the ODE solution "clamped" C(f) to keep C(f) as used in the reaction rate calculation within known limits, even if the ODE solution average had driften outside those limits.  The consequence of such a clamp is an internal inconsistency in the model, whereby the beta-averaged C(f) no longer equals the average calculated by solving the ODEs.  That can lead to unexpected and likely unphpysical changes in reaction rate during the simulations.  We found that clamps were frequent for blend_fs and will discuss that more later.
