**Equations**

To predict the course of mixing-limited reactions using a closure model, we will integrate differential equations for the average amount of each species:

$$\frac{dC}{dt}=\Sum{\nu_{ij}r_{j}}$$

**How to select the limits to use for closure**

The closure problem for fast reactions between two separate streams arises because we do not know the (product of the) instantaneous concentrations needed to calculate the average reaction rate.  Turbulent fluctuations in concentration, that is the temporal fluctuations in concentration at a fixed location, require estimation/ modeling.  The combustion community and later Baldgya and others use the concept of mixture fraction and its variance to represent the fluctuations.  Mixture fraction is a conserved scalar with value 1.0 in stream 1 and 0.0 in stream 2.  When there is no chemical reaction, the relationship between species concentration (average or instantaneous) and mixture fraction is given by the "mixing line" for each species, shown here for the 5-reaction azo-coupling at typical concentrations - A is fed in stream 1 with B and C fed in stream 2:

![Mixing line when no reaction occurs](/blog/docs/assets/Mixing_line.png){: width="400" }

Turbulent fluctuations in the local value of f may be described statistically using the mean and variance of f and the beta proability density function (pdf) is a good representation that is commonly used.  When the streams first meet, the variance is total, with essentially no contact with molecules from the other stream.  The variance is reduced by mixing on various scales, in a cascade from larger to smaller eddies; as variance reduces, the pdf gets tighter and eventually becomes a delta function at the mean of f, corresponding to a completely mixed system with no concentration fluctuations. The mixing-sensitivity of chemical reactions occurs when the reactions are fast enough to take place at least partually while mixing is still in progress.

For the 5-reaction version of azo-coupling, the reactions are as follows:
```
"reactions": [
  "R1: A + B -> R",
  "R2: A + R -> S",
  "R3: A + B -> T",
  "R4: A + T -> S",
  "R5: A + C -> Q"
  ],
  
  "rate_constants": {
    "R1": 12238,
    "R2": 1.835,
    "R3": 921,
    "R4": 22.25,
    "R5": 124.5
  },
```

The mixing line allows calculation of the number of molecules (or moles) of each substance that would be present at any location if no reaction occurred.  That is one extreme limit that approximates the situation when reaction kinetics are very slow.  There is another extreme that describes behaviour when kinetics are infinitely fast; in that case, reactants from the streams cannot co-exist, as any collision would lead to immediate product formation.  For the 5-reaction azo-coupling with all reactions assumed to be instantaneous and taking place together:

![All reactions at maximum extent](/blog/docs/assets/all_at_maximum_extent.png){: width="400" }

At this particular limit, A cannot co-exist with either B or C; and neither R nor T may be found in the product solution as both are immediately converted to S.

Baldyga's proposed closures estimate C(f) for any species based on interpolation between the mixing line and a limiting case with infinitely fast kinetics, using the average species concentration as a weight:

$$C_{i}(f) = C_{i}^{inf}(f)+[C_{i}^{0}(f)-C_{i}^{inf}(f)]\frac{\overline{C_{i}}-\overline{C_{i}^{inf}}}{\overline{C_{i}^{0}}-\overline{C_{i}^{inf}}}$$

This results in the blue shaded line C(f) for component C interpolating between the mixing line (shown dashed) and the infinitely fast reaction limit (coloured black), taken from a snapshot during simulation of the 5-reaction system:

![Example C(f) interpolation](/blog/docs/assets/Example_Cf.png){: width="400" }

This plot also shows the beta pdf (in light grey) with values shown on the secondary y-axis, centred around the average f of 0.0625 in this example, taking on a near-normal distribution as mixing proceeds to completion.  The flat lines are the average concentration from the ODE solver and a beta-integrated C(f), both of which should have the same value.
